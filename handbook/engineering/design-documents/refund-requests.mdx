## Briefing

EU consumer law (Consumer Rights Directive 2011/83/EU) gives B2C buyers a 14-day
**right of withdrawal** on distance contracts. For digital goods this right can be
waived — but only under strict conditions. To comply, merchants need a way for
customers to *request a refund*, and Polar needs to expose a **refund button** to
EU B2C customers where it's legally mandatory.

The fear is the opposite failure mode: a naive
"customer clicks → auto-refund" flow lets anyone download the product and then
claim their money back, getting the digital good for free. So the design has to
do two things at once: **comply with the law** without **opening a free-product
loophole**.

This document maps what exists today, the legal principle that resolves the
tension, and a phased plan.

## Goals

- A customer-initiated **refund request** is recorded as a support case (audit
  trail), even on the happy path.
- EU B2C customers see a **refund button** wherever the law makes it mandatory.
- Merchants are protected from abuse via a **waiver opt-in** captured at checkout
  ("immediate access ⇒ you waive your 14-day withdrawal right").
- Refunding pulls back the **specific benefit** that was purchased.
- Merchants get **signals** (refund history per customer, notes, optional
  blocklist) to manage repeat abusers.

## Non-goals (for v1)

- Automatic, no-merchant-in-the-loop refunds for arbitrary products.
- Withdrawal-right handling for benefit types we cannot track or revoke
  (Discord, custom) beyond requiring an up-front waiver.
- A full customer reputation/risk-scoring engine.

## The legal principle (this is the whole design)

Under the Directive, for digital content the merchant **loses the obligation to
refund** only if **all three** hold:

1. The consumer gave **prior express consent** to begin access before the 14 days
   elapse, **and**
2. The consumer **acknowledged they thereby lose** the right of withdrawal, **and**
3. The merchant **confirmed** that consent on a durable medium.

That is exactly the "waiver opt-in upon purchase." It produces **two compliant
regimes**, and the loophole only appears if you mix them:

| Regime | Condition | Refund button | Loophole? |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| **Waiver path** | Waiver given → immediate access granted | Discretionary once **accessed** | No — they waived |
| **No-waiver path** | No waiver | **Mandatory** within 14 days, *regardless of access* | Only if you granted access anyway |

> **The one rule that closes the loophole:**
> **Never grant access without either a recorded waiver or accepting the 14-day refund liability.**
> i.e. "Have it now if you waive, or wait 14 days."

Everything below exists to make that rule enforceable.

**Takeaway:** the refund engine and the case primitive are done. The missing
pieces are all *upstream of the button* — consent capture, access tracking, and
selective revocation — which is exactly why they must land first.

## Plan

### Phase 0 — Foundations

The button is the *last* thing built. These three remove the loophole and the
risk before any customer can self-serve a refund.

1. **Consent / waiver capture at checkout.**
   - Detect EU B2C (`is_business_customer` + billing country).
   - Waiver opt-in: *"Give me immediate access. I acknowledge I lose my 14-day
     withdrawal right once I access the content."*
   - Store the consent **with its text version + timestamp** on the order —
     durable-medium proof is itself a legal requirement, not just nice-to-have.

2. **Unify an `accessed` predicate per benefit grant.**
   - Wrap existing signals (download count, license activation).
   - Explicitly classify benefit types that **cannot** report access
     (Discord/custom) — the policy engine treats those differently.

3. **Selective benefit revocation** (merchant endpoint, per order/product) so a
   refund pulls back exactly what was purchased, not everything the customer owns.

### Phase 1 — The refund-request case

4. Implement the `refund_request` case type, mirroring `dispute_case.py`: a
   `RefundRequestSupportCase` subclass with a FK to `order`, plus a handler service.
5. Build the **customer-facing case UI** (net-new portal surface). This also
   unblocks customer-visible disputes later — the `customer` audience already
   exists in the model.

### Phase 2 — The button + policy engine

6. **Eligibility engine** computing `mandatory` vs `discretionary`:
   `mandatory = EU_B2C AND within_14_days AND NOT (waived AND accessed)`.
7. Surface the refund button to the customer **only when eligible**. On click it
   opens a `refund_request` case, executes the refund, and revokes the
   specific benefit. Gated behind `org.can_refund()` + Phase 0 in place.

### Phase 3 — Abuse controls

8. **Per-customer refund metrics** — derivable today by filtering the refund
   table on `customer_id`; surface "N refund requests across your products."
9. **Customer notes** (net-new field).
10. **Email / card blocklist** — net-new, or lean on Stripe Radar + the existing
    `fraudulent` refund reason (which already warns it blocks customers).

---

## Happy-path decision

Record **every** customer-initiated refund as a `refund_request` case, even when/if
it auto-resolves. It's cheap (the primitive exists) and the case history *is* the
per-customer abuse signal Phase 3 needs. Auto-close on the happy path; keep the
trail.
